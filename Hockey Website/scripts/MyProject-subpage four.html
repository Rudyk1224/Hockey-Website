The Rules of Hockey

Penalties are what constitute the rules of hockey. Penalties occur when a player breaks the rules of hockey, and is punished by sitting in the penalty bench. Depending on the severity of the penalty and the intent, players can sit in the penalty box for either 2 minutes, 4 minutes, 5 minutes, or be suspended for the entire rest of the game. When a player from a team commits a penalty, the other team gains a 1 player advantage. The team who took a penalty now has 1 less player on the ice, resulting in a 1 player advantage for the other team (i.e: team A takes a penalty, so now it is a 5 players vs 4 players advantage for the other team). It is important that if the same team takes another penalty, then it is a 2 man advntage for the other team (i.e: 5 players v 3 players).

The team who takes a penalty (or the team with the less amount of players) is now on the "penalty kill". The penalty kill is a hockey term describing the team with less players that must "kill off" the minutes of the penalty (i.e: 2 minutes) without surrendering a goal.

The team who draws a penalty (the team with more amount of players) is now on the "powerplay" The powerplay describes the team with the advantage that has a better chance of scoring a goal, due to having more players. If a goal is scored on the powerplay, the remaining minutes on the penalty will be erased and the player serving the penalty will be released (to go back to regular 5 on 5 play).



Anyways, let's look at the different types of penalties:



Penalty Description Number of Minutes Picture
Boarding When a player hits an unexpecting player from behind into the boards 2 minutes (minor penalty) or 5 minutes (major penalty)
Charging When a player skates very fast from a good distance, to launch a bodycheck into another player 2 minutes (minor penalty) or 5 minutes (major penalty)
Cross-checking When a player holds the stick in front of themselves in a horizontal position, and forcefully pushes the opponent with the stick 2 minutes (minor penalty)
Delay of Game When a player purposefully attempts to stall the game. This penalty is usually assessed when a player flips the puck over the glass and out of play or when the goalie knocks the net over to snub the opponent out of a high danger scoring opportunity 2 minutes (minor penalty)
Elbowing When a player drives their elbow into another player 2 minutes (minor penalty)
Slashing When a player swings their stick at an opposing player. Often times, slashing is caused by a forceful "chop" motion to an opponent's stick or gloves 2 minutes (minor penalty)
Roughing When a player uses unecessary force or violence on another player (oftentimes a punch) 2 minutes (minor penalty)
High-sticking When a player's stick comes up too high on an opponent and makes contact (usually with their face). If blood is drawn as a result of high-sticking, the penalty changes from 2 minutes to 4 minutes 2 minutes or 4 minutes (minor penalty)
Kneeing When a player attempts a hit, but ends up using their knee as the main point of contact with the opponent. Kneeing is one of the most dangerous incidents in hockey and can easily cause torn MCLs and ACLs 2 minutes (minor penalty) or 5 minutes (major penalty)
Holding When a player grabs onto the opponent (usually with their arms or hands) to restrict/slow down their movement 2 minutes (minor penalty)
Interference When a player makes unecessary contact with an opposing player that doesn't have the puck. Goalie interference is when a player makes contact with the goalie that impairs the goalie's ability to move freely 2 minutes (minor penalty) or 5 minutes (major penalty)
Hooking When a player uses the stick to slow down an opposing player (usually by lifting up the opponent's stick for too long) 2 minutes (minor penalty)
Tripping When a player purposefully trips the opponent (usually with their stick) to slow down the opposing player. Tripping is one of the most common penalties in hockey 2 minutes (minor penalty)
